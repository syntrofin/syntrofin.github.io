Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM)

A Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM) is the ultimate defense against scope creep. It proves that every single line of code you write maps directly back to a C-level business goal. If a feature doesn't trace all the way up to the Vision, it gets cut.

As an architect, this is your master blueprint—a careful orchestration of standard architecture phases.

Why this makes you an elite Architect

If a developer comes to you during the Build phase (Step 9) and asks, "Can we just build a quick custom Lightning Web Component instead of a Flow for the intake form?"...You look at the matrix. You know that the Vision (Step 1) is to scale quickly, and the Governance (Step 10) requires the Ops team to maintain this long-term. Your answer is: "No. We are using standard Screen Flows so the business admins can update the intake questions next year without a developer." Every technical decision is shielded by the business justification above it.

The Golden Rule of Traceability

If you read the matrix from Bottom to Top, you should be able to say the word "Because" between every row.

  • Why do we have a validation rule? Because we built a Screen Flow.

  • Why a Screen Flow? Because we need an external portal.

  • Why a portal? Because vendors need to enter their own data.

  • Why do they need to enter their own data? Because Ops is wasting 4 hours per PDF.

  • Why does that matter? Because the Vision is to reduce onboarding time by 50%.

The Architect's Move

The true power of this framework is that it forces a chronological sequence. It prevents developers from writing code before the data model is secure, and it prevents business stakeholders from demanding features that do not align with their strategic KPIs.

Stage 1: Vision and Pre-Discovery (The Anchor)

Stop the scope creep before it starts.

  • Phase 1: Vision (The 'Why')

    • The Goal: Define the single Strategic KPI that determines project success.

    • The Architect's Move: If a requirement does not directly improve this KPI, politely reject it or push it to Phase 2.

  • Phase 2: Pre-Discovery

    • The Goal: Establish the Process Taxonomy (Level 1 to Level 3).

    • The Architect's Move: Use the "Straw Man" approach. Present a predefined list of in-scope processes to the executive steering committee and force them to explicitly agree on the boundaries.

Stage 2: Pre-Discovery Leads to Discovery (The Diagnosis)

Find the root cause of the business pain.

  • Phase 3: Discovery (The 'As-Is')

    • The Goal: Document the current Pain Point.

    • The Architect's Move: Never trust what a user says; trust what they click. Make the Subject Matter Expert (SME) share their screen and demonstrate the broken process live.

  • Phase 4: Business Intent (The 'To-Be')

    • The Goal: Define the Future State in plain English, completely independent of technology.

    • The Architect's Move: Use "What If" nudges (e.g., "What if the system just did this for you?") to guide the SME toward standard Salesforce capabilities without using technical jargon.

  • Phase 5: Gap Analysis (The Delta)

    • The Goal: Break the Identified Gap into Process, System, and Data deficits.

    • The Architect's Move: This is the pivot point. If you only identify a System Gap but ignore the Process Gap, your technical solution will fail upon user adoption.

Stage 3: Gap Analysis Leads to Solution (The Blueprint)

Design the Salesforce architecture to bridge the gaps.

  • Phase 6: Solutioning (The Concept)

    • The Goal: Define the Process Architecture and Configurations.

    • The Architect's Move: Determine your architectural pivot. Are you forking the data at the Order object? Are you shifting calculations to an OmniStudio Pricing Procedure?

  • Phase 7: Systems (The Landscape)

    • The Goal: Identify the required Infrastructure and managed packages.

    • The Architect's Move: Call out the specific clouds and licenses required (e.g., Revenue Cloud Advanced, Salesforce Billing, Experience Cloud).

  • Phase 8: Data (The Info Arch)

    • The Goal: Map the Data Model and Development.

    • The Architect's Move: Define the specific custom objects, required standard objects, and the relational mapping (e.g., Master-Detail vs. Lookup) that will support the logic.

Stage 4: Solution Leads to Transformation (The Execution)

Build, test, and protect the investment.

  • Phase 9: Integration (The Connective Tissue)

    • The Goal: Define the System Interfaces.

    • The Architect's Move: Identify inbound and outbound data flows, batch schedules, API types, and error-handling fallbacks for external systems (like an ERP).

  • Phase 10: Development (The Build)

    • The Goal: Write the Technical Specs (Declarative vs. Programmatic).

    • The Architect's Move: Hand the Tech Lead the "What" and the "Why" through perfectly structured Jira tickets (Given/When/Then formats). Ensure declarative tools (Flows, OmniStudio) are prioritized over custom code (Apex).

  • Phase 11: UAT (User Acceptance Testing)

    • The Goal: Execute the Business Validation Script.

    • The Architect's Move: Do not hand the business a blank sandbox. Give them a strict, step-by-step testing script mapped directly to the original Phase 4 Business Intent.

  • Phase 12: Governance (The Guardrails)

    • The Goal: Establish Rules & Maintenance to prevent regression.

    • The Architect's Move: Implement technical locks (like Advanced Approvals or Validation Rules) and process audits (Center of Excellence reviews) to ensure users cannot bypass the new architecture.

Fork at the bridge (Order):

That is an incredibly sharp observation. You caught the exact architectural pivot point. "Forking at the bridge"—specifically, the Order object—is the secret to making Split Billing work cleanly in Salesforce Revenue Cloud.

If you fork the process too early or too late, the entire architecture collapses. Here is why you made the right call to design it this way:

1. Why we don't fork at the Quote (Too Early)

If you tell a sales rep they have to build three separate Quotes just because the parent company wants three separate invoices, the reps will mutiny. They want to present one single, unified Proposal Document to the buyer. By keeping the Quote unified and using Quote Line Groups to assign the Billing_Account__c, you protect the Sales UX.

2. Why we don't fork at the Invoice (Too Late)

If you send a single, massive Order to the Billing engine and try to write custom code to chop it up into multiple Invoices, you are playing with fire. You risk breaking tax calculations, proration, and general ledger (GL) posting rules.

3. Forking at the Order (The "Goldilocks" Zone)

The Order object sits perfectly in the middle. It translates what Sales sold into what Finance needs to collect. By utilizing Salesforce CPQ's native "Order By" functionality in Phase 10 of our MDST template, the system evaluates that single Quote, sees three different Billing Accounts on the line groups, and automatically spins up three separate Orders.

Because Salesforce Billing generates Invoices directly from Orders (a 1:1 or Many:1 relationship), those three Orders natively generate three separate Invoices. No custom Apex needed for the split.

You are thinking exactly like a Principal Architect—you found the path of least resistance through the standard data model.

Harvest Documents Before Discovery Begins

This is the "blank page" problem of enterprise consulting. How do you know what a company does before they tell you what they do?

Methods to Compile Initial Process Catalogue

Here are the widely used methods and tools architects use to compile that initial L1-L3 Process Catalog before Discovery begins.

Method 1: Industry Reference Models (The Cheat Codes)

You don't need to guess what an event management, manufacturing, or software company does. Industry consortiums have already mapped every business process on earth. You download their map, highlight what you think applies, and use that as your baseline.

  • APQC (American Productivity & Quality Center): This is the gold standard. APQC provides free, downloadable "Process Classification Frameworks" (PCFs) for almost every industry. It gives you the exact L1 to L4 taxonomy for a business.

  • TM Forum (eTOM): If you are working in Telecommunications or Media, this is the mandatory standard for process mapping.

  • ITIL: For IT service management processes.

Method 2: Artifact Harvesting (The Paper Trail)

Before you schedule a single workshop, you ask the client's IT or Project Management Office (PMO) for existing documentation. You mine these documents strictly for nouns and verbs to add to your catalog.

  • The SOW / RFP: The Statement of Work or Request for Proposal that initiated your project is a goldmine. If the SOW says "Must support split-billing," you immediately add L3: Split-Billing to your catalog.

  • Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) & Training Manuals: HR and onboarding teams often have step-by-step guides for new hires. You aren't reading them for the pain points; you are just extracting the titles of the processes.

  • Existing Org Charts: If you see a team called "Deal Desk," you know you have to add L3: Non-Standard Pricing Approvals to your catalog.

Method 3: System Snooping (The Metadata Recce)

If the organization is already using Salesforce (or another ERP/CRM) and you are coming in to implement Revenue Cloud Advanced, their current system configuration will tell you exactly what processes they run today.

  • Custom Objects & Fields: If you look at their Salesforce schema and see a custom object called Vendor_Rebate__c, you immediately add L3: Vendor Rebate Management to your map.

  • Approval Processes & Flows: Looking at the names of active workflows gives you a literal list of automated business processes.

  3. The Technical Blueprint: The specific Revenue Cloud/OmniStudio components the dev must use (this stops them from going rogue).

  4. Acceptance Criteria (Given / When / Then): The exact testing scenarios they must pass before you will review their code.

Jira Ticket Example: Automated Margin Floor Enforcement

Epic: EPC & Pricing Strategy Phase 1

Issue Type: Story

Status: Ready for Dev

Assignee: [Developer Name]

1. User Story

As a Sales Representative,

I want the system to automatically uplift my core package price (e.g., Booth Space) if my blended margin drops below the approved category floor due to variable costs (e.g., Catering),

So that I can generate profitable quotes instantly without manual Excel calculations.

2. Business Context & Value

  • Current Pain: Reps are manually calculating margins; quotes are frequently closing below the 20% floor.

  • Traceability: This fulfills the CFO's requirement to eliminate margin erosion on multi-product events and reduces quote turnaround time by 15 minutes per deal.

3. Technical Blueprint (Architect Notes)

Do not use Apex or custom triggers for this logic. This must be built using standard OmniStudio components to ensure long-term declarable maintenance.

  • Data Model Constraints: * Source variable costs from the standard Cost__c object via the active Cost Book.

    • Source the target margin floor (e.g., 20%) from the custom Margin_Targets Decision Matrix.

  • Build Architecture:

    • Modify the active Pricing Procedure (Event_Pricing_v2).

    • Add a Lookup Action to fetch the variable cost.

    • Add a Calculation Step utilizing a Formula Element:

      • Logic: If (Net Price - Total Cost) / Net Price < Target_Margin, execute price uplift on the primary line item to reach the Target_Margin.

4. Acceptance Criteria (BDD Format)

Scenario 1: Standard deal meeting the margin floor

  • Given a Quote with a "Booth Space" ($10,000 List) and "Catering" ($2,000 Cost).

  • When the pricing engine calculates the quote.

  • Then the margin calculates at 80% and no price uplift is applied. The Net Price remains $10,000.

Scenario 2: Deal breaching the margin floor (The Uplift)

  • Given a Quote with a "Booth Space" ($7,500 List) and "Catering" ($6,500 Cost), creating a sub-20% margin.

  • When the pricing engine calculates the quote.

  • Then the system references the Decision Matrix (20% floor for Events) AND automatically uplifts the Booth Space Net Price to $8,125 to satisfy the 20% margin requirement.

Scenario 3: Negative Testing (Missing Cost Data)

  • Given a Quote includes a product that is missing a Cost__c record in the Cost Book.

  • When the pricing engine runs.

  • Then the procedure defaults the cost to $0, calculates the margin, and flags a custom checkbox Missing_Cost_Data__c = TRUE for reporting.

Why this ticket is bulletproof

  1. It separates the "What" from the "How." The User Story tells them what the sales rep needs. The Technical Blueprint tells them how you expect the architecture to look.

  2. It forces BDD (Behavior-Driven Development). By writing the Acceptance Criteria in "Given/When/Then" format, you have practically written the QA team's test scripts for them. The developer knows exactly what math needs to work before they hit "Submit for Pull Request."

  3. It handles the edge cases. Scenario 3 is where most CPQ projects fail—nobody tells the developer what to do when data is missing. You caught it in the ticket.

When you hand a developer a ticket like this, you aren't just assigning work; you are governing the architecture.

Jira Ticket Example: Margin Exception Approval Routing

This is the second half of the technical solution. You built the engine to calculate the correct price; now you must build the guardrails to protect it.

If you do not write this second ticket, a sales rep will simply look at the newly uplifted $8,125 Booth Space price, manually type in a 10% discount to bring it back down, and completely bypass the margin floor you just engineered.

Here is the exact Jira ticket to configure the Advanced Approvals architecture that acts as the final safety net.

Epic: EPC & Pricing Strategy Phase 1

Issue Type: Story

Status: Ready for Dev

Assignee: [Developer Name]

1. User Story

As the VP of Sales,

I want the system to automatically lock a quote and require my explicit approval if a sales representative manually applies a discretionary discount that drops the blended margin below the algorithmic floor,

So that I can prevent margin erosion and review unprofitable deals before they are sent to the customer.

2. Business Context & Value

  • Current Pain: Reps are discounting packages without understanding the underlying catering/event costs, and leadership has no visibility until the deal is already won.

  • Traceability: This fulfills the Governance requirement of our architecture. It ensures that 0% of deals close under the 20% margin target without executive sign-off, protecting the CFO's profitability KPI.

3. Technical Blueprint (Architect Notes)

Do not use standard Salesforce native approvals. This must be built using the Revenue Cloud Advanced Approvals (sbaa) managed package to allow for parallel routing and smart approvals in Phase 2.

  • Data Model & Logic: * Create a custom formula field on the Quote: Margin_Delta__c (Calculated Margin vs. Target Margin from the Decision Matrix).

    • Configure an Approval Rule targeting the Quote object.

    • Configure an Approval Condition: Fire when Margin_Delta__c is less than 0.

  • Build Architecture:

    • Create an Approver record for the VP of Sales.

    • Link the Approval Rule to the VP of Sales Approver record.

    • Ensure the Approval Actions are configured to lock the Quote record (status = 'Pending Approval') and hide the "Generate Document" button while the quote is locked.

4. Acceptance Criteria (BDD Format)

Scenario 1: Rep accepts the system-calculated price (No Approval Needed)

  • Given a Quote where the Pricing Procedure has calculated the Net Price to satisfy the 20% margin floor.

  • When the sales rep saves the quote without applying any additional manual discounts.

  • Then the Approval Status remains 'Approved' (or bypasses the rule), and the rep can generate the proposal document immediately.

Scenario 2: Rep attempts to bypass the floor (Approval Triggered)

  • Given a Quote where the system calculated an $8,125 Net Price to protect the margin floor.

  • When the sales rep manually applies a 10% line-level discount, dropping the total margin to 18%.

  • Then the Quote Status changes to 'Pending Approval', the "Generate Document" button is hidden, and an Advanced Approval email is routed to the VP of Sales.

Scenario 3: Smart Approvals / Re-submission

  • Given a Quote that was previously approved by the VP of Sales at an 18% margin.

  • When the sales rep modifies the quote further, dropping the margin down to 15%.

  • Then the system invalidates the previous approval and re-triggers the Approval Rule, requiring a fresh sign-off from the VP of Sales.

The Architect's Advantage

By writing these two tickets back-to-back, you have completely mapped the technical execution of a complex business problem. The first ticket handles the Automation, and this ticket handles the Governance. When you hand this to your dev team, there is zero ambiguity about how the system should behave.

Sequencing JIRA Tickets By Working With Development Lead

You absolutely can and should. In fact, if you try to split and sequence the sprint tickets entirely in a vacuum without the Technical Lead or Senior Developer, the project is almost guaranteed to hit a wall.

You are bringing up a crucial distinction in enterprise delivery: the difference between Logical Architecture and Physical Execution.

As the Solution Architect, you own the "What" and the "Why." The Tech Lead owns the "How" and the "When." Here is exactly how that partnership works and why their knowledge is the missing puzzle piece in Sprint 0.

The Division of Labor

When you sit down to split the work, you are combining two completely different types of expertise.

The Architect Brings (The Blueprint) The Tech Lead Brings (The Hammer)
Business Value: Knows that the 20% margin rule must be unbreakable to satisfy the CFO. Platform Limits: Knows that putting too many Calculation Steps in one OmniStudio Procedure will hit governor limits.
Cross-System Impact: Knows that the Cost Book must be fed by the Financial ERP. Data Skew: Knows how to structure the batch API job to prevent locking errors when updating 10,000 cost records.
The "Happy Path": Defines how the quote should ideally flow for the sales rep. The Edge Cases: Asks, "What happens if two reps try to edit the same quote at the exact same time?"
Acceptance Criteria (Business): "The quote must lock if margin < 20%." Acceptance Criteria (Technical): "Test class must achieve 85% coverage and handle bulkified triggers."

How the "Split" Actually Happens (The Handshake)

You do not write the granular Jira tickets alone. You bring the Epic and the Technical Blueprint to the Tech Lead, and together, you carve it into Sprint-sized slices.

Here is what that conversation sounds like for our "Dinner & Drinks" margin problem:

  • You (The Architect): "We need an OmniStudio Pricing Procedure that fetches the catering cost, does the math, and uplifts the Booth Space price to hit a 20% margin. I have the business requirements right here."

  • The Tech Lead: "Okay, looking at the complexity of that math, that is too big for one developer to build and test in a single sprint. We need to split this into three tickets."

    • Ticket 1: Dev A builds the DataRaptor Extract to pull the Cost data.

    • Ticket 2: Dev B builds the Decision Matrix for the target margins.

    • Ticket 3: Dev C (the Senior Dev) builds the actual Pricing Procedure that calls the elements from Dev A and Dev B.

  • The Result: You ensure the business rule is intact. The Tech Lead ensures the work is modular, assignable, and won't break the build.

Why You Need Them for Sequencing

Developers know dependencies better than anyone. You might think it makes sense to build the Pricing Procedure in Sprint 1 to show the business some quick progress.

The Tech Lead will immediately stop you: "No, we can't build the Procedure until the custom fields for Internal_Cost__c and Margin_Delta__c are deployed to the sandbox. Data model comes first, UI comes last."

The "Three Amigos" Principle

In mature Agile teams, this splitting exercise is often called the "Three Amigos" meeting. It involves:

  1. The Product Owner / Architect: Represents the business intent.

  2. The Tech Lead: Represents the technical reality.

  3. The QA Lead: Represents the testing strategy.

When the three of you sit in a room during Sprint 0 to groom the backlog, the tickets that come out are bulletproof. You ensure the right problem is being solved, the Dev ensures it can actually be built, and QA ensures it can be proven.

"Sprint 0 Handoff" With Development / Tech Lead

This is the critical transition point. You are passing the baton from the conceptual to the physical.

If you just email the Tech Lead a link to a Confluence page and say, "Let me know if you have questions," they will build what they think you want, not what you actually designed.

To prevent that, you run a highly structured Sprint 0 Handoff (often called the "Three Amigos" kickoff). Here is the exact agenda to ensure your Revenue Cloud architecture is sliced into perfect, actionable development sprints.

The Sprint 0 Tech Lead Handoff Agenda

Duration: 90 Minutes

Attendees: Solution Architect (You), Technical Lead, QA Lead.

Goal: Translate the Logical Architecture into a sequenced Jira Backlog.

Part 1: The North Star & The Box (15 Mins)

The Architect leads this. You must ground the developers in the business reality before they start thinking about code.

  • The Briefing: "We are solving the 'Dinner & Drinks' margin erosion problem. The CFO's mandate is that 0% of event quotes close below a 20% margin without VP approval."

  • The Boundaries: "For Phase 1, we are only touching the Cost__c object, configuring a Decision Matrix, and modifying the Event_Pricing_v2 Pricing Procedure. We are strictly ignoring contract generation or billing for now."

The Architect presents the blueprints. You hand over the specific artifacts we built earlier.

  • Data & Integration: Walk through the 3D Gap Analysis and Data Template. "We need a nightly batch to pull the $2,500 catering costs from the ERP. Tech Lead, what is our volume risk here?"

  • The Engine (OmniStudio): Show the Technical Blueprint from your Jira ticket. "We need a Lookup Action feeding into a Formula Element. If the margin drops below 20%, it triggers a Calculation Step to uplift the Booth Space price."

  • The Guardrails: Explain the Advanced Approvals requirement. "If a rep manually discounts the new price, the system locks."

Part 3: The "Slicing" Session (30 Mins)

The Tech Lead takes the wheel. This is where the magic happens. They take your massive architectural Epics and chop them into 2-week Sprint tickets.

  • Tech Lead Action: They look at your OmniStudio requirement and split it based on platform reality:

    • Ticket A (Sprint 1): Build the Custom Metadata / Decision Matrix for Margin Targets.

    • Ticket B (Sprint 1): Create the custom fields (Internal_Cost__c, Margin_Delta__c).

    • Ticket C (Sprint 2): Build the actual Pricing Procedure Formula Element.

  • Architect's Role: You listen and validate. If the Tech Lead suggests putting the Advanced Approval rule in Sprint 1, you push back: "We can't test the approval routing until the Pricing Procedure actually outputs a Net Price in Sprint 2. Let's move Approvals to Sprint 3."

Part 4: Definition of Ready (DoR) & Acceptance Criteria (15 Mins)

The QA Lead takes the wheel. You ensure the tickets are actually testable.

  • The Check: The QA lead looks at your BDD Acceptance Criteria (Given/When/Then).

  • The Negotiation: "Architect, you said 'System automatically uplifts the price'. Tech Lead, how are we logging that uplift so QA can verify it happened?" * The Output: The Tech Lead agrees to add a hidden checkbox (System_Uplift_Applied__c) to the Quote Line strictly so QA can run their automated testing scripts against it.

The Artifact Handover

At the end of this 90-minute meeting, you officially hand over the keys. You provide the Tech Lead with:

  1. The finalized Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM).

  2. The drafted Jira Epics (with your Business Value and Technical Blueprints attached).

  3. The UAT Script (so the developers know exactly how the business will grade their work).

A Rough Sprint Mapping

This is where most enterprise projects fall apart. A firm will do brilliant architectural design, and then blindly throw it into a two-week Scrum cycle, resulting in what we call "Wagile"—Waterfall planning disguised as Agile execution.

If you just hand a developer the entire "Automated Margin Safeguarding" epic and say, "See you in two weeks," they will fail. Revenue Cloud requires a strict sequence of operations because the UI depends on the rules, the rules depend on the data model, and the data model depends on the integration.

Here is how you map the Syntrofin architectural playbook into a formal, sequential Agile Sprint structure.

The Agile RLM Delivery Methodology

This structure assumes standard 2-week sprints. As the Solution Architect, your job is to keep the dev team focused strictly on the layer of the architecture assigned to that sprint.

Sprint 0: The Runway (Architecture & Backlog)

Unlike developers, the Architect does the heavy lifting in Sprint 0. You cannot build a house while you are still pouring the foundation.

  • The Goal: Define the boundaries and write the tickets.

  • Playbook Mapping: Phases 1, 2, and 3 (Pre-Discovery, Workshops, Gap Analysis).

  • Key Artifacts Delivered: * Signed-off L1-L3 Process Taxonomy.

    • The Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM).

    • A fully groomed Jira backlog with Acceptance Criteria written in Given/When/Then format.

  • The Architect's Rule: No code is written in Sprint 0. If the business cannot agree on the 20% margin rule here, you do not let Sprint 1 begin.

Sprint 1: The Foundation (Data & Integration)

You cannot build a dynamic Pricing Procedure if the system doesn't know what things cost.

  • The Goal: Stand up the data architecture and external system connections.

  • Playbook Mapping: Phase 3 (Data & Integration Templates).

  • Sprint Backlog (What Devs Build):

    • Configure the core standard objects: Product2, PricebookEntry, and Cost__c.

    • Build the Decision Matrix structure (Target Margin % by Product Family).

    • Integration: Configure the nightly batch API to ingest catering/inventory costs from the Financial ERP into the Salesforce Cost Book.

  • Sprint Review Demo: Show the VP of Sales that the system now holds accurate, real-time cost data.

Sprint 2: The Core Engine (OmniStudio & Pricing)

Now that the data exists, you build the machine that does the math.

  • The Goal: Automate the margin calculations and price uplifts.

  • Playbook Mapping: Phase 3 (System & Development Templates).

  • Sprint Backlog (What Devs Build):

    • Build the Pricing Procedure (Event_Pricing_v2).

    • Configure the Lookup Action to fetch the active cost from Sprint 1.

    • Configure the Formula Element and Calculation Step to execute the uplift if the margin drops below 20%.

  • Sprint Review Demo: Build a quote live in front of the Pricing Ops team (Sarah). Show the system automatically adjusting the Booth Space price to absorb the catering cost spike.

Sprint 3: The Guardrails (Governance & Approvals)

The engine works, now you build the brakes to stop sales reps from bypassing it.

  • The Goal: Enforce the business rules on the newly automated pricing.

  • Playbook Mapping: Phase 3 (Governance Template) & Architect's Jira Ticket #2.

  • Sprint Backlog (What Devs Build):

    • Install and configure Advanced Approvals (sbaa).

    • Create the custom formula field (Margin_Delta__c).

    • Build the Approval Rule routing negative deltas to the VP of Sales.

    • Configure Quote UI rules to hide the "Generate Document" button when locked.

  • Sprint Review Demo: Log in as a sales rep, manually discount the price to 15% margin, and show the quote locking. Log in as the VP of Sales and approve it.

Sprint 4: Hardening & UAT

The build is complete. Now you bring the business in to try and break it.

  • The Goal: Business sign-off and deployment readiness.

  • Playbook Mapping: Phase 4 (UAT Validation Script).

  • Sprint Backlog (What Devs Build):

    • Defect resolution (fixing whatever bugs the business finds during UAT).

    • Finalizing deployment packages and sandbox-to-production migration scripts.

  • The Architect's Role: You run the UAT sessions. You hand the business the exact UAT script we wrote earlier and watch them execute the Given/When/Then scenarios.

Why this Agile sequence is non-negotiable

If you let a developer build the Advanced Approvals (Sprint 3) before the Cost Data is integrated (Sprint 1), the approvals will fire randomly because the system is doing math with blank variables. By sequencing the sprints as Data -> Engine -> Guardrails, you ensure every sprint produces a shippable, demo-able increment of value.
