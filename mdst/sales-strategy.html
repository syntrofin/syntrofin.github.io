Slow Adoptors. Pain of staying the same is greater than the cost of changing
("The Lag") 		Current state vs Future state: The larger the gap, the more value you have created. If there is no gap, there is no sale. Gap Selling The fundamental premise is that change is hard, and people only buy when the pain of staying the same is greater than the cost of changing. A problem-centric sales methodology that shifts the focus away from the product and entirely onto the "gap" between a customer's Current State and their desired Future State. Gap Selling by Jim Keenan
Active Evaluation High-Competition Markets (e.g., Telecom, Consumer Electronics)
("The Long Evaluation") 		Toch Point Selling (CDJ) Used for high-competition industries (e.g., Electronics, Software). If a company’s data shows that customers spend a long time in "Active Evaluation," the selling model shifts from outbound pitching to providing expert content and comparison tools to win the "evaluation war." The Customer Decision Journey (CDJ): The "Touch-Point" Model by McKinsey
The Loyalty Loop Subscription/SaaS Models (e.g., Cloud services, Netflix-style B2B)
("The Repeat") 		Toch Point Selling (CDJ) This model is used for companies that rely on recurring revenue (e.g., SaaS, Insurance, Consumer Goods). It suggests that the "post-purchase" experience is the most effective "selling" situation because it triggers the "Loyalty Loop," bypassing the need for a new "Awareness" phase. The Customer Decision Journey (CDJ): The "Touch-point" Model by McKinsey
Extreme Touch Point (Zero-Friction) Consumption/Usage-Based Models (e.g., Supercharging, Vendor Wi-Fi, APIs)
("The Invisible Sale") 		Zero-Friction Utility This model relies on behavioral engineering to eliminate the "Active Evaluation" phase entirely. The goal is to make the purchasing and consumption process so effortless (e.g., auto-billing, plug-and-play) that the user forms a habit, effectively simulating a monopoly because the friction to switch is too high. The Hook Model (Nir Eyal) & Frictionless Commerce (McKinsey / Scott Galloway)
The Direct Sale (D2C / B2C) High-Volume Transactional Retail (e.g., Tesla Web Orders, Event Tickets, E-commerce)
("The Impulse Buy") 		Direct Touch Point (CDJ) Used when selling directly to the end-consumer where you own 100% of the customer journey. The strategy is entirely marketing-led, relying on UI/UX optimization, social proof (FOMO), and seamless digital checkout experiences (like 1-Click or Apple Pay) to drive immediate conversions without human sales interaction. The Customer Decision Journey (CDJ): The "Touch-point" Model by McKinsey
The Channel Sale (B2B2C / Dealer Track) Wholesale & Distributor Models (e.g., Home Depot Water Tanks, Dealership Networks)
("The Supply Chain Sale") 		Channel Enablement Used when you sell to an intermediary who then sells to the end-user, resulting in fractured control of the customer journey. The strategy shifts away from end-user psychology toward operational ease—creating frictionless B2B touch points like automated EDI restocking, volume rebates, and seamless partner portals to keep the dealer loyal to your brand. Channel Strategy & Supply Chain Optimization (Classic MBA Frameworks)
For Startups
("The High Growth") 		Start-up / High Growth Sales Acceleration Formula Often follow the Sales Acceleration Formula. The model focuses on "Sales Hiring" and "Lead Management" as a predictable engineering problem rather than individual talent. The Sales Acceleration Formula: Using Data, Technology, and Inbound Selling to go from $0 to $100 Million, written by Mark Roberge
For Disruptive Market
("The Disruption") 		Status Quo Resistance, Selling a disruptive tech where "doing nothing" is your biggest competitor. Challenger Sale The Challenger Model is preferred. It assumes the customer is stuck in "status quo" and needs a salesperson to "teach" them why their current way of doing business is costing them money. Modern Strategy: The Challenger Sale by Matthew Dixon.
