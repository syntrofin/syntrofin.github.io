A Day in Life Discovery

In the real world, especially when dealing with complex platforms like Salesforce CPQ or Revenue Cloud, SMEs don't separate their current pain from their future desires.

When you ask them about how they handle a midterm amendment today (the As-Is), they will immediately complain about the manual credit memos and jump straight into how they want it automated tomorrow (the To-Be). And as an architect, when they look to you and ask, "Well, how does the system handle this?" they are forcing a real-time Gap Analysis—your excellent term, the "pre-gap." Here is how to embrace that chaotic reality and organize Discovery so it actually works.

The idea that Discovery is a neat, chronological march from "As-Is" to "To-Be" to "Gap Analysis" is a theoretical fallacy.

1. Organize by "Commercial Thread," Not by Phase

Stop scheduling "As-Is Workshops" and "To-Be Workshops." Instead, organize your Discovery sessions by the Business Process or Revenue Thread (exactly like the "One-Time" vs. "Subscription" sprints we discussed earlier). If the workshop is focused on the "Renewal Lifecycle," you let the conversation flow naturally through the past, present, and future of just that topic.

2. The In-the-Room "Micro-Cycle"

When you are facilitating, guide the SMEs through a continuous loop for every specific capability they bring up. You are running As-Is, To-Be, and Pre-Gap simultaneously in a 10-minute window:

  • The Pain (As-Is): "We currently use three spreadsheets to calculate proration.”
  • The Intent (To-Be): "We need the system to just do the math automatically when a rep changes the quantity.”
  • The Pre-Gap (Platform Steering): This is where you leverage your architecture expertise. Instead of just writing down their To-Be, you guide it. "Revenue Cloud handles this out-of-the-box using the standard Pricing Procedure and Assetization rules, provided we standardize your service periods. Are you open to adopting the standard platform calculation?”

3. Managing the "Pre-Gap" (Platform-Guided Design)

When business leaders ask, "What are our options using the platform?", they are giving you a massive gift. They are inviting you to shape their To-Be state to fit standard Salesforce architecture. In management consulting and enterprise architecture, this is often called "Platform-Driven Business Design." You aren't just taking orders; you are advising them on best practices.

  • If their To-Be aligns with standard functionality: You confirm it immediately (closing the gap on the spot).
  • If their To-Be requires heavy customization: You use the pre-gap to challenge them. "We can build that custom logic, but if we adjust your process slightly to fit the standard Product Selling Model, you save 200 hours of development and make future maintenance drastically cheaper.”

4. The Unified Discovery Matrix (Documentation)

Because the conversation happens all at once, your documentation must capture it all at once. Ditch the separate As-Is and To-Be documents. Use a unified Capability Matrix. For every row (e.g., "Mid-Term Seat Add-Ons"), you have these columns side-by-side:

  • Current State (The Pain): Manual calculation, prone to errors.
  • Business Intent (The Goal): Automated, accurate prorated quoting.
  • Platform Recommendation (The Pre-Gap/Architecture): Utilize standard CPQ/RLM Amendment functionality; requires standardizing subscription term fields.
  • Gap / Risk: High risk if legacy data cannot be migrated cleanly into the new asset structure.

Extend L1-3

Compiling the L1-L3 list before Discovery is exactly how you lock down the project scope. If a specific process is on the L3 list, it is in scope; if it isn't, it is out of scope. With L1 mapping to the massive Value Stream, L2 to the major Process Area buckets, and L3 to the Specific Process tasks, we have a rock-solid foundation to hand over to a Scrum Master. Here is exactly how you bridge that Process Catalog into standard Jira/Agile issue types to maintain your architectural control while keeping the Agile team moving horizontally across the Revenue Cloud lifecycle.

The RTM to Agile Mapping Framework

RTM Hierarchy (The Architecture View) Agile/Jira Issue Type (The Delivery View) Purpose & RLM Example
L1: Value Stream Initiative The massive, end-to-end lifecycle. Example: Quote-to-Cash (QTC).
L2: Process Area Component / Theme The major buckets within the Value Stream. Used for reporting and filtering in Jira. Example: Product Configuration, Pricing.
L3: Specific Process (Process Catalog) Epic The actual task to be accomplished. This is the business capability boundary. It groups the commercial threads. Example: Subscription Amendments or Event Quoting.
L4: The Commercial Thread (Pre-Gap Steering) User Story This is the "Transaction-Based" narrative. It defines how the L3 capability is executed across the RLM platform for a specific scenario. Example: "As a Rep, I need to co-term a mid-year SaaS add-on so that the Proration Rule accurately feeds the Sales Order."
L5: Architectural Gap / Risk Acceptance Criteria (DoD) The rules that prove the User Story survives the transaction. Example: "Quote Line successfully converts to an AssetStatePeriod."
L6: Technical Build Task Sub-Task The specific administrative actions assigned to developers to satisfy the story. Example: "Configure ProductSellingModel record," "Build PricingProcedure step."

Why L3 as the "Epic" is Your Best Tool

By making the L3 Process Catalog item the Jira Epic, you keep the business stakeholders perfectly comfortable. When a business leader looks at the Jira board, they see Epics named exactly what they asked for during project kickoff and pre-discovery planning. However, underneath that L3 Epic, you and the Scrum Master populate the L4 User Stories strictly as Commercial Threads (Horizontal Progress), not isolated objects (Vertical Progress). For example, if the L3 Epic is "Vendor Onboarding":

  • Story 1: Standard hardware vendor onboarding flow (from catalog to ERP).
  • Story 2: Complex software vendor onboarding flow (with termed revenue schedules).
This satisfies the Scrum Master's need for actionable, sprint-sized tickets while guaranteeing that the Salesforce CPQ and Billing engines are configured sequentially and logically.

The Architect's "Cheat Sheet”

At the end of the day, Agile Jira boards are just a mechanism for distributing and tracking work. The magic happens when you, as the lead architect, shape those work containers so they force the development team to build the system correctly. When your Epics are business capabilities (the L3 Process Catalog) and your Stories are End-to-End Revenue Threads, you completely eliminate the risk of developers building isolated, useless features. But to make this truly bulletproof, your Business Analysts (BAs) need to write Acceptance Criteria (L5) that actively prevent developers from slipping back into bad habits. Here are the guidelines you can hand to your BAs to ensure every story adheres to Revenue Cloud best practices.

The Architect's "Cheat Sheet" for BAs: Writing RLM Acceptance Criteria

When drafting the L5 Acceptance Criteria (the Definition of Done) for any Product, Pricing, or Configuration story, BAs must enforce the following three rules: 1. The "No Dead Ends" Rule (Mandate the Transaction) A developer cannot write a story to simply "create a product field." The criteria must force that field through the transaction engine.

  • Bad AC: "The 'Hardware Type' field is visible on the Product record."
  • RLM-Ready AC: "The 'Hardware Type' field is visible on the Product record AND correctly maps to the Quote Line Item when added to a Standard Quote."
2. The "Engine Callout" Rule (Name the RLM Component) Salesforce Revenue Cloud is a math and rules engine. BAs should not write generic pricing requirements; they must specifically name the RLM component that handles the logic.
  • Bad AC: "The system should apply a 10% discount for volume."
  • RLM-Ready AC: "The Pricing Procedure evaluates the volume quantity and correctly generates the Net Price on the Price Waterfall."
3. The "Downstream Proof" Rule (The Lifecycle Check) Every subscription or service story must prove it can survive beyond the initial sale. This prevents the "Siloed Sprint" trap.
  • Bad AC: "The user can sell a 12-month software license."
  • RLM-Ready AC: "The 12-month software license successfully converts from a Sales Order into an Asset-trackable record with the correct start and end dates for future renewals."

Why This Empowers Your BAs By giving your BAs this framework, you are essentially deputizing them to enforce your architectural vision. They become the gatekeepers. If a BA writes a story that doesn't push the data through to an Order or Asset, the Scrum Master will kick it back during backlog refinement because it doesn't meet the "Definition of Ready."
