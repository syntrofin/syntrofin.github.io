Where Pre-Discovery Fits

After Vision: Pre-Discovery (Project Initiation)

What you are doing: Building the Process Catalog (The "Forest"). At this stage, before you interview a single sales rep, you sit with leadership to map out the high-level business capabilities. You are not mapping how they do it; you are just listing what they do. We break this into levels.

Hierarchy What It Is Example for our "Thrive" Scenario When It's Defined
Level 1 (L1) Value Stream: The massive, end-to-end lifecycle. Quote-to-Cash (QTC) Project Kickoff (Scoping)
Level 2 (L2) Process Area: The major buckets within the Value Stream. Product Configuration, Pricing, Contracting, Billing. Project Kickoff (Scoping)
Level 3 (L3) Specific Process: The actual task to be accomplished. Event Quoting, Margin Approval, Vendor Onboarding. Pre-Discovery Planning

Architect's Secret: Compiling this L1-L3 list before Discovery is how you define project scope. If "Vendor Onboarding" is on the L3 list, it's in scope. If "Employee Payroll" isn't on the list, it's out of scope.

Leading to: The Discovery Phase

What you are doing: Documenting the "As-Is" (The "Trees"). Now you take your compiled L3 list into the business workshops. You are no longer just listing processes; you are mapping the literal steps, pain points, and actors for each one.

  • You say: "We are here to look at process L3.2: Event Quoting. Walk me through exactly what you click, what spreadsheets you open, and who you email to get a quote out the door today."

  • The Output: This is where you populate the Discovery Template and the 3D Gap Analysis we built earlier. You identify that the Event Quoting process is broken because it relies on manual margin calculations.

Realized Through: Solutioning, and Build

What you are doing: Designing the "To-Be". You take the broken As-Is processes and redraw them to leverage the new technology (like Salesforce Revenue Cloud).

  • The Output: A completely new process map where the system handles the heavy lifting (e.g., the system calculates the 20% margin, not the rep).

Governed After: Post Go Live

What you are doing: Maintaining the Process Catalog. The list of processes you compiled in Stage 1 is now your Center of Excellence (CoE) backlog. When the business asks for a new feature, you look at the Process Catalog and ask, "Which L3 process does this improve?"

Summary: The Timeline of Process Compilation

  1. Before you start: You list the Names of the processes to build boundaries (The Scope).

  2. During Discovery: You document the Current Reality of those processes (The As-Is).

  3. During Solutioning: You design the Future State of those processes (The To-Be).


Harvest Documents Before Discovery Begins

This is the "blank page" problem of enterprise consulting. How do you know what a company does before they tell you what they do?

The golden rule of the Pre-Discovery (Recce) phase is: You never start with a blank page. You do not wait for the business to recite their processes; you bring a predefined list and force them to edit it.

Methods to Compile Initial Process Catalogue

Here are the widely used methods and tools architects use to compile that initial L1-L3 Process Catalog before Discovery begins.

Method 1: Industry Reference Models (The Cheat Codes)

You don't need to guess what an event management, manufacturing, or software company does. Industry consortiums have already mapped every business process on earth. You download their map, highlight what you think applies, and use that as your baseline.

  • APQC (American Productivity & Quality Center): This is the gold standard. APQC provides free, downloadable "Process Classification Frameworks" (PCFs) for almost every industry. It gives you the exact L1 to L4 taxonomy for a business.

  • TM Forum (eTOM): If you are working in Telecommunications or Media, this is the mandatory standard for process mapping.

  • ITIL: For IT service management processes.

Method 2: Artifact Harvesting (The Paper Trail)

Before you schedule a single workshop, you ask the client's IT or Project Management Office (PMO) for existing documentation. You mine these documents strictly for nouns and verbs to add to your catalog.

  • The SOW / RFP: The Statement of Work or Request for Proposal that initiated your project is a goldmine. If the SOW says "Must support split-billing," you immediately add L3: Split-Billing to your catalog.

  • Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) & Training Manuals: HR and onboarding teams often have step-by-step guides for new hires. You aren't reading them for the pain points; you are just extracting the titles of the processes.

  • Existing Org Charts: If you see a team called "Deal Desk," you know you have to add L3: Non-Standard Pricing Approvals to your catalog.

Method 3: System Snooping (The Metadata Recce)

If the organization is already using Salesforce (or another ERP/CRM) and you are coming in to implement Revenue Cloud Advanced, their current system configuration will tell you exactly what processes they run today.

  • Custom Objects & Fields: If you look at their Salesforce schema and see a custom object called Vendor_Rebate__c, you immediately add L3: Vendor Rebate Management to your map.

  • Approval Processes & Flows: Looking at the names of active workflows gives you a literal list of automated business processes.

The Tools of the Trade

When compiling and presenting this catalog, architects use a mix of high-tech visualization and low-tech organization tools.

1. Process Mining Tools (The High-Tech Approach)

In highly mature enterprise environments, organizations use software that connects to their ERP/CRM and reads the system logs to automatically draw the process maps based on what users are actually clicking.

  • Celonis: The heavyweight champion of process mining.

  • UiPath Process Mining: Often used if the company is already heavy into robotic process automation (RPA).

2. Enterprise Architecture (EA) & Mapping Tools

These are used to visually draw the L1-L3 taxonomy boxes so executives can agree on the boundaries.

  • Lucidchart / Visio: The standard for drawing the high-level Value Streams (L1) and Process Areas (L2).

  • Signavio (SAP) / ARIS / LeanIX: Heavy-duty Enterprise Architecture tools that link processes to IT systems and business capabilities.

  • Elements.cloud: Specifically built for Salesforce. It allows you to map business processes and tie them directly to Salesforce metadata (like fields and objects).

3. The Humble Spreadsheet (The Workhorse)

Despite all the fancy tools, 90% of architects compile their final Process Catalog in Excel or Smartsheet.

  • Why? Because a spreadsheet allows you to add columns for "In Scope / Out of Scope", "Process Owner", and "Priority." It is the easiest format to present to a steering committee for sign-off before Discovery begins.

The "Day 0" Alignment Workshop

Once you have used APQC and Artifact Harvesting to build your draft spreadsheet, you hold a 2-hour "Day 0" meeting with the executive sponsors.

You put the spreadsheet on the screen and say:

"Based on industry standards and your existing documentation, here are the 40 processes that make up your Quote-to-Cash motion. Are any of these obsolete? Are we missing any major capabilities?"

They will cross out five, add two, and you walk out with your locked, baseline catalog. Now, you are ready for Discovery.


Emai To Ask For Pre-Discovery Documents

Here is the exact email template you can send to the project sponsor.

As an architect, this email does two things: it proves you are already doing the heavy lifting before the clock starts, and it sets the expectation that your workshops will be highly targeted, not open-ended venting sessions.

I have tailored this specifically for a Revenue Cloud engagement under your firm, so it is ready to copy, paste, and send.

Email

Subject: Pre-Discovery Artifact Request: [Client Name] Revenue Cloud Transformation

Hi [Sponsor Name],

I am looking forward to our upcoming official kickoff for the Revenue Cloud implementation.

To ensure we make the absolute best use of your team's time during the upcoming Discovery workshops, my team at Syntrofin is currently compiling the baseline "Quote-to-Cash" process catalog (Level 1 through Level 3 processes).

Rather than starting from a blank page in our workshops, we want to map as much of your current process landscape as possible beforehand. This allows us to use our workshop time to focus purely on pain points, edge cases, and future-state solutioning, rather than basic fact-finding.

Could you or your PMO please provide any existing documentation in the following categories by [Date - ideally 4-5 days before Day 0]?

1. Strategic & Scope Alignment

  • The finalized Business Case or Project Charter (focusing on KPIs like margin targets or quote velocity).

  • Any existing high-level Enterprise Architecture or System Landscape diagrams.

2. Organizational Structure

  • Current Org Charts for Sales, Sales Operations, Deal Desk, and Finance.

  • A list of key subject matter experts (SMEs) who own the quoting, pricing, and approval processes.

3. Process & Operational Artifacts

  • Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) or new-hire training manuals for the sales/quoting teams.

  • Any existing process maps (Visio, Lucidchart, etc.) covering CPQ, order management, or billing.

  • A copy of your current manual quoting tools (e.g., the Excel spreadsheets currently used for margin calculations).

4. System Context (If available)

  • A data dictionary or schema export of your current CRM/ERP setup (specifically around Products, Price Books, and custom quoting objects).

Please don't worry if some of these documents are outdated or incomplete—we are simply looking for the "nouns and verbs" of how your business operates today to build our initial process map. You can upload everything directly to this secure folder: [Link to Google Drive / SharePoint].

Let me know if you have any questions.

Best regards,

Ravi Gandavarapu Architect, Syntrofin LLC

Why this email works:

  1. The "Why" is client-centric: You aren't asking for homework; you are telling them you want to save their team from sitting in long, boring baseline meetings.

  2. It requests the "Excel Spreadsheet": Specifically asking for their current manual tools (like the margin calculator) is the ultimate cheat code for understanding their L3 pricing processes before you even talk to a rep.

  3. It lowers the barrier to entry: Telling them "it's okay if it's outdated" ensures they actually send it, rather than waiting three weeks to update a Visio file.


Process Taxonomy (Level 1 - Level 3) - RLM

That is spot on. "Reconnaissance" or "scouting" is the absolute best analogy for the Pre-Discovery phase.

If you just wander into the process forest without scouting it first, the business stakeholders will drag you deep into the weeds, and you will spend three weeks mapping how they order office supplies instead of fixing their quoting engine.

Here is how your analogy perfectly maps to the architectural lifecycle:

1. Pre-Discovery: The Aerial Recce (Drawing the Map)

You are flying over the forest in a helicopter. You are not looking at individual trees or leaves. You are identifying the boundaries of the forest, the major rivers, and the mountains.

  • The Goal: Define the perimeter.

  • The Output: A Level 1 to Level 3 Process Taxonomy.

  • What it sounds like: "Okay, we are going to fix 'Event Quoting' and 'Vendor Intake.' We are strictly ignoring the 'Employee Payroll' process over there in the valley."

2. Discovery: Boots on the Ground (Walking the Trails)

Now that you have the map, you land the helicopter and walk the specific trails you outlined during the recce. This is where you look at the actual trees.

  • The Goal: Find the obstacles on the path.

  • The Output: The "As-Is" pain points and the Gap Analysis.

  • What it sounds like: "Alright, we are walking the 'Event Quoting' trail today. Show me exactly where you get stuck in the mud. Ah, I see you are manually calculating a 20% margin in Excel here—that's a hazard we need to pave over."

3. Solutioning: Engineering the Highway (The "To-Be")

Why the "Recce" is your ultimate shield as an Architect

The most powerful thing you can do as a consultant is control the scope.

When a sales rep inevitably brings up a totally unrelated complaint during a Discovery workshop (e.g., "Our lead routing is broken!"), you pull out your map from the Pre-Discovery recce and say:

"I hear you, and that is a real pain point. But if we look at our Process Taxonomy for this phase, Lead Routing is outside our perimeter. We are strictly scouting the 'Quote-to-Cash' territory right now. Let's put Lead Routing in the backlog for the next recce."

Taxonomy For Salesforce CPQ and Revenue Cloud implementation

  • At 10,000 feet (Level 1 & Level 2): The platform doesn't matter. A business must execute "Quote-to-Cash" (L1) and "Pricing Strategy" (L2) whether they are using Salesforce Revenue Cloud Advanced, an Oracle ERP, or a notebook. These are pure business capabilities.

  • At the ground level (Level 3): Knowing you are moving to Revenue Cloud Advanced absolutely changes your recce. Because Revenue Cloud Advanced utilizes OmniStudio, Enterprise Product Catalog (EPC), and dynamic Pricing Procedures, it can natively handle things that legacy CPQ cannot. Knowing this allows you to scout for very specific L3 processes (like attribute-based pricing or matrix-driven margin floors) because you know your future platform can easily digest them.

How to use this "Map" in your Discovery Kickoff

When you sit down with the VP of Sales and the CFO, you put this exact taxonomy on the screen and draw physical boxes around what you are going to scout.

The Boundary Setting: "Looking at our QTC map, for Phase 1, we are exclusively focused on resolving the margin leakage. Therefore, our discovery sessions will only cover 2.3 (Margin Floor Calculations), 3.3 (Product Configuration), and 4.2 (Approval Routing). We are completely ignoring 5.3 (Contract Redlining) and 6.4 (Renewals) until Phase 2. Does everyone agree?"

Why knowing "Revenue Cloud Advanced" changes the L3 Map

If you were just implementing standard Sales Cloud, your L3 process for pricing might just be "Select Price Book."

But because you know you have Revenue Cloud Advanced (and OmniStudio), you intentionally add L3 processes like 2.2 (Attribute-Based Pricing) and 2.3 (Margin Floor Calculations) to your map. You scout for those complex requirements because you know the platform's Pricing Procedures and Decision Matrices can solve them efficiently. You are hunting for the exact problems your machine was built to fix.
