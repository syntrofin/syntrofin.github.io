In Discovery, you identify and classify sales models.

Where this sits in the RLM phase stack

Discovery answers what exists and what must be supported.
Design answers how each model will be implemented.

So:

  • ❌ “Let’s redesign sales motion” → Not Discovery
  • ✅ “These sales motions exist and must be supported” → Discovery

What “sales model” means in RLM Discovery

A sales model is defined by:

  • How demand is created
  • How price is constructed
  • How orders are placed
  • How revenue is recognized

Not by org charts.

Examples of sales models you identify in Discovery

During Discovery, you explicitly surface questions like:

1. Who sells and how

  • Direct sales
  • Inside sales
  • Partner / reseller
  • Self-service / digital

2. How pricing is constructed

  • List → discount
  • Contracted pricing
  • Usage-based pricing
  • Tiered / volume pricing
  • Bundle-based pricing

3. How customers buy

  • One-time purchase
  • Subscription
  • Usage / consumption
  • Hybrid (subscription + usage)
  • Amendment-heavy vs straight-through

4. How revenue is recognized

  • Point-in-time
  • Over-time
  • Milestone-based
  • Variable consideration

(This is critical in RLM.)

5. How fulfillment happens

  • Immediate digital delivery
  • Physical shipment
  • Project-based delivery
  • Ongoing service

Discovery deliverable (important)

Discovery produces a Sales Model Inventory, not solutions.

Example output:

“The platform must support:

  • Direct enterprise subscription sales with amendments
  • Partner-led resale with net pricing
  • Usage-based add-ons billed monthly
  • One-time services recognized on milestones”

No config decisions yet.

    • A duplex
    • A mixed-use building

You are not drawing the house yet —
you are deciding what kinds of buildings must legally stand on this land.

If sales models are discovered late:

  • CPQ redesign

This is the #1 cause of RLM program overruns.

This tension you’re pointing to is real, structural, and not a personal failure. The mistake many make is treating it as a moral contradiction instead of a systems transition.

I’ll explain this in a way that helps reconcile it without cynicism and without naivety.

1. The idealist model they grew up with (why it made sense)

Most of the current generation was educated on a normative model of the world:

  • Fairness
  • Equality
  • Meritocracy
  • Voice and inclusion
  • Transparency
  • “Do the right thing and outcomes will follow”

This model is:

  • Morally correct
  • Educationally useful
  • Necessary for social trust

It answers:

What should a just world aim to be?

But it is not a system design.

2. The shock: encountering hierarchical, biased systems

Corporate systems are:

  • Hierarchical
  • Power-distributed, not fairness-distributed
  • Biased by incentives, not ideals
  • Path-dependent (history matters)
  • Politically negotiated, not logically pure

This feels like betrayal, but it’s actually category mismatch.

Corporates answer a different question:

How do large groups coordinate action under uncertainty and risk?

Hierarchy is not moral failure — it is a coordination technology.

3. The key reconciliation: ideals ≠ operating model

The mental unlock is this:

Ideals define direction.
Systems define constraints.

You don’t abandon ideals when entering imperfect systems;
you apply them locally, incrementally, and strategically.

Trying to impose ideals globally and instantly inside a large system fails — not because ideals are wrong, but because systems resist sudden rewrites.

4. Three phases most people go through (and get stuck in)

Phase 1: Moral shock

  • “This is unfair”
  • “Why do politics matter more than merit?”
  • “Why isn’t this transparent?”

This phase is accurate but incomplete.

Phase 2: Cynical adaptation (the danger zone)

  • “That’s just how it is”
  • “Play the game or lose”
  • “Ideals are for talk, not reality”

Here, people gain effectiveness but lose meaning.

Phase 3: Strategic idealism (the mature reconciliation)

  • Understand power structures clearly
  • Accept constraints without endorsing them
  • Move systems one decision at a time

This is where effective leaders live.

5. A useful reframe: corporations are not moral agents

Corporations are:

  • Incentive machines
  • Risk management engines
  • Coordination platforms

Expecting them to be “fair” in a human sense is like expecting a traffic system to be kind.

But:

  • You can design safer roads
  • You can advocate better rules
  • You can drive ethically within the system

6. What individuals can realistically do (without self-betrayal)

1. Shrink the battlefield

Don’t try to fix “the company”.
Fix:

  • Your team
  • Your project
  • Your decisions

Justice scales locally, not globally.

2. Learn power without worshipping it

  • Understand incentives
  • Map stakeholders
  • Read the room

This is not selling out — it’s literacy.

3. Replace “fair outcomes” with “fair process”

Outcomes will always be biased.
Processes can be improved.

This is where real impact happens.

4. Detach identity from system behavior

The system’s flaws are not your moral failure.

You are not the company.

7. A metaphor that helps

Think of ideals as north stars, not destinations.

  • You navigate by them
  • You never fully reach them
  • Without them, you drift
  • If you try to jump straight to them, you crash

8. A line that often resonates with younger professionals

The world is not fair — but it is influenceable.
Maturity is learning where, when, and how.

Hold ideals as direction, accept systems as terrain, and act where leverage exists.
