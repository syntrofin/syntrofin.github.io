Recognizing Smallness to Vastness — Thanking Old and Striving for New

One day, a curious traveler wandering through a vast garden grew tired and sat beneath the wide, cooling shade of an enormous Banyan Tree. As he rested, he noticed a Sage and told him he has been travelling from far away lands and explain Hinduism in a visual way.

The Sage smiled and replied that this single tree held a strange and wonderful truth.

He invited the traveler to observe closely. The Banyan Tree had no single visible beginning and no clear end. From its branches, finger-like roots descended from the sky, embracing the earth. Once they touched the soil, they transformed into strong new trunks. The tree was always ancient because of its deep roots, yet always new because of these ever-forming pillars.

“This,” the Sage said, “is how Hinduism lives.”

What many call “one God” is, in Hindu thought, the Ultimate Reality — infinite, uncountable, and beyond numbers. One or two are human inventions; the infinite cannot be measured.

The Elements of the Banyan Tree and Hinduism

The Vedas are the tree’s earliest seed — emerging not as rigid commandments, but as revelations that gave rise to everything that followed. Like spores that no longer need to be seen once the tree has fully grown, their essence lives on throughout the whole.

Humanized expressions of cosmic stories are Devas — often translated as “gods,” though imperfectly — are the tree’s main stem. They are humanized expressions of cosmic principles, nature, memory, and history, blended with heroic narratives to make the abstract understandable.

Ever-evolving guides for human conduct are the branches. They grow, bend, and adapt with time, culture, and circumstance.

Varying and the countless temples are the Banyan’s prop roots — touching the ground in many places, offering support and access from every direction.

Symbolic actions performed in temples and homes — rituals, gestures, chants — are its leaves, quietly sustaining life.

Six schools of philosophy are the fruit, containing hidden flowers within — logic, inquiry, and reflection — feeding those who seek understanding.

Forces of Reality

The Sage then spoke of the known and unknown forces of reality:

  • Vishnu represents preservation — the ongoing balance that keeps the tree alive.
  • Shiva represents transformation — stillness and change, destruction and renewal.
  • Shakti represents energy and nature — the subtle power that makes growth possible.
  • Brahma, the creator of the visible world, represents secondary creation.

Conclusion: The Birds in the Branches

The curious traveller now knows that the Banyan tree perfectly illustrates how Hinduism lives as Recognizing Smallness to Vastness. The abstract forces take on thousands of visible forms to support the tree and the life within it.

And Hindus, he said, are like the birds who live in its branches: small sparrows and strong hawks alike. They read the stories, perform rituals, visit temples, question, doubt, believe, and belong — each in their own way — resting within the same vast tree. Hindus Live Thanking Old and Striving for New.

What is Hinduism in an Intellectual Way

One Take Away

Infinite → many windows → personalized surrender.

Because this question is most often framed from an Abrahamic faith reference point, it is best clarified in relation to that framework. If there is only one takeaway, it is this.

Abrahamic approach: One God → one relational focus → mass surrender.
Hindu approach: Infinite → many windows → personalized surrender.

Meditation Core

Hindu rituals are meditation at core.

Hindu rituals are meditation in action — sensory, symbolic, and concrete — whereas Upanishadic meditation is meditation in thought: abstract, formless, and inward.

Hindu rituals, temples, and festivals can be understood as concrete, embodied, representational forms of meditation, in contrast to abstract, formless meditation like dhyāna or jñāna practices in the Upanishads. Let’s break it down carefully.

Two “types” of meditation

Type How it works Example in Hinduism Strength
Abstract / Internal Focus entirely on mental objects, concepts, or the self. Minimal sensory anchors. Upanishadic meditation on Atman/Brahman, dhyāna on the self, contemplation of Neti-Neti. Direct insight, liberation-oriented, subtle
Concrete / Representational Engage the body, senses, symbols, and actions. Mental focus is scaffolded by ritual objects, sounds, and gestures. Yajña (fire offerings), temple puja, mantra recitation, festivals, bhakti practices Anchors attention, evokes devotion, creates repeated neural patterns, accessible to most people

Why concrete meditation works

  • Embodied focus: Postures, hand gestures (mudras), offerings, circumambulations → body reinforces mind.
  • Sensory scaffolding: Fire, incense, bells, chants → stabilizes attention naturally.
  • Symbolic representation: Each deity, mantra, or ritual object becomes a focal point for concentration (like a meditation object).
  • Emotional engagement: Devotion and joy are built in, making the practice sticky and memorable.
  • Social reinforcement: Festivals and temple gatherings amplify the effect through shared experience.

Essentially: Concrete rituals train the mind the way abstract meditation trains the mind, but via action, sense, and symbol rather than pure thought.

Temple Puja vs. Dhyāna

Aspect Abstract (Dhyāna) Concrete (Puja)
Focus Brahman, Atman, inner self Deity form, mantra, ritual action
Engagement Mental only Body, senses, speech, mind
Discipline Internal attention External + internal attention
Outcome Insight, liberation Devotion, focus, moral/emotional refinement
Accessibility Requires mental maturity Accessible to wide age & skill range

Notice how puja is like a multi-sensory meditation shortcut. Even a child or someone new to spiritual practice can concentrate deeply — the mind is “locked in” via senses and symbolic actions.

Why abstract meditation came later

  • The Upanishads emphasized liberation and knowledge.
  • Abstract meditation works best when:
    • Practitioner has some mental discipline
    • The mind can focus without external scaffolds
  • But most people historically didn’t have that access, so concrete rituals remained vital.

In effect: rituals and temples are practical meditation systems embedded in culture, while Upanishadic meditation is refined, abstracted, and direct.

Belief

Hindu practices are belief-compatible, not belief-dependent.

Practical Test, Let’s see when you visit a temple -

A Hindu temple does not ask:

  • What do you believe?
  • Do you accept this theology?
  • Do you affirm this god as the only truth?

It asks only:

  • Remove shoes
  • Wash hands
  • Enter respectfully
  • Participate or observe

You can:

  • doubt
  • reinterpret
  • suspend belief
  • treat the deity symbolically and still participate fully.

That is not true in belief-mandatory systems.

A critical test

Ask this question: If belief disappears, does the practice still function?

Functions regardless of belief:

  • Meditation → yes
  • Yoga → yes
  • Fasting → yes
  • Temple visit → yes
  • Festival participation → yes
  • Ethical vows → yes

Now compare:

  • Creed recitation without belief → often invalid
  • Sacrament without belief → often void

A more accurate statement:

Instead of saying: Hindu practices are belief-based, A more precise statement is:Hindu practices are belief-compatible, not belief-dependent. That one sentence is the key.
