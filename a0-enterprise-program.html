Breakdown of Framework


Widely Accepted Stack and Breakdown of Maturity, Approach and Transformation Framework for Revenue Lifecycle / Enterprise Platform

  1. Vision & Intent
  2. Discovery
  3. Design
  4. Build
  5. Validate
  6. Deploy
  7. Operate & Evolve

Assess current stage: Use data-driven evaluation against benchmarks.
Identify gaps and set goals: Compare performance to industry best practices to find areas for improvement and set targets.

1. Vision & Intent

  • Business goals
  • Revenue model intent
  • Guardrails (regulatory, financial, risk)
  • Success measures

Why are we building this at all?

2. Discovery (most misunderstood phase)

Discovery is fact-finding and constraint identification, not solutioning.

Includes:

  • Business process reality (not slides)
  • Contract types & pricing behaviors
  • Revenue recognition rules
  • Legal & compliance constraints
  • Data truth (what exists vs what’s trusted)
  • Org & operating model realities

Deliverable:

  • Discovery Framework / Baseline
  • Known unknowns surfaced

What is actually true today?

Implement changes and automate: Introduce new processes and technologies to address weaknesses.

3. Design

  • Target operating model
  • Process orchestration
  • System responsibilities (CRM / CPQ / Billing / ERP)
  • Integration patterns
  • Data ownership

Deliverable:

  • Target Architecture

How should it work?

4. Build

  • Configuration
  • Customization
  • Integrations
  • Data models

Make it real.

5. Validate

  • SIT / UAT
  • Revenue & accounting validation
  • Edge-case testing

Does it work under stress?

6. Deploy

  • Cutover
  • Data migration
  • Training
  • Go-live

Put people in it.

Monitor and adapt: Continuously track KPIs and perform reviews for ongoing improvement.

7. Operate & Evolve

  • Monitoring
  • Enhancements
  • Regulatory changes
  • Scale & optimization

Live in it.

Who Actually Contributes (reality, not theory)

1. Executive Sponsor (required)

Usually one of:

  • CFO (very common for ERP / RLM)
  • CRO / Chief Revenue Officer
  • COO

They:

  • Own why the program exists
  • Define success criteria
  • Provide authority

Without this role, vision is weak or tactical.

Strategy consulting (why-change vision)

Examples:

  • McKinsey, BCG, Bain
  • Tier-2 strategy boutiques

They help answer:

  • Why now?
  • What business outcomes matter?
  • How revenue model or operating model should evolve?

This is business vision, not IT vision.

Used when:

  • Large transformation
  • Market or revenue model shift
  • M&A-driven complexity

2. Business Leadership (co-authors)

Functional heads from:

  • Sales
  • Finance
  • Legal / Compliance
  • Operations

They:

  • State business pain and goals
  • Set guardrails (compliance, risk)
  • Agree on what “good” looks like

This is where commercial intent comes from.

Business / Transformation consulting (operating model vision)

Examples:

  • Accenture, Deloitte, PwC, EY
  • Transformation boutiques

They help:

  • Translate strategy into operating intent
  • Align Sales, Finance, Ops
  • Define guardrails

This is where Revenue Lifecycle Vision often emerges.

3. Enterprise Architecture / Lead Architect (critical)

Often invisible but essential.

They:

  • Keep vision at the right abstraction
  • Prevent solution leakage
  • Ensure vision is implementable
  • Translate business language → system-neutral intent

A good architect will say:

“That’s design, not vision.”

Enterprise Architecture consulting (structure & abstraction)

Examples:

  • EA boutiques
  • Senior independent architects
  • Internal EA teams with advisors

They:

  • Keep vision system-neutral
  • Prevent tool bias
  • Ensure future scalability

This role is often underappreciated but crucial.

4. IT Leadership (contributors, not owners)

  • CIO / IT Director
  • Platform owners

They:

  • Provide feasibility input
  • Highlight constraints
  • Estimate scale and complexity

They do not define vision.

Vendor / platform consulting (should not define vision)

Examples:

  • Salesforce SIs
  • ERP vendors

They:

  • Provide feasibility input
  • Share best practices

But when vendors define vision:

  • The vision becomes the product
  • Scope is distorted

Typical Flow

  1. Business senses a problem or opportunity
  2. ELT mandates exploration
  3. Vision workshop(s) happen
  4. Vision statement is ratified
  5. Discovery begins
  6. Design, build, deploy follow

How this usually plays out in real companies

Common (good) pattern

  1. Strategy / transformation consultant frames why
  2. Business leaders align on outcomes
  3. EA helps articulate vision cleanly
  4. Vendors are brought in after vision

Very common (problematic) pattern

  1. Vendor brought in early
  2. Vision becomes “implement X cloud”
  3. Discovery is rushed
  4. Rework later

Why companies still use consultants (even strong ones)

Because:

  • Internal leaders lack time
  • Neutral facilitation is needed
  • Cross-functional alignment is hard
  • Someone has to ask uncomfortable questions

Consultants provide structure and air cover.

Consulting partners

Good consultants:

  • Facilitate workshops
  • Ask uncomfortable questions
  • Help articulate the vision clearly

Bad consultants:

  • Insert their product as vision
