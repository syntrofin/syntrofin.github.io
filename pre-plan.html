Deciding what sales models to include in the salesforce RLM project part of discovery 

Yes — deciding which sales models to include is absolutely part of Discovery in a Salesforce Revenue Lifecycle (RLM) program.  But there’s an important precision to add:  In Discovery, you identify and classify sales models.  You do not design or optimize them yet.  Let me frame this cleanly so it holds up in exec rooms and interviews. 

Where this sits in the RLM phase stack 

Discovery answers what exists and what must be supported.  Design answers how each model will be implemented.  So: 
  ‣ ❌ “Let’s redesign sales motion” → Not Discovery 
  ‣ ✅ “These sales motions exist and must be supported” → Discovery 

What “sales model” means in RLM Discovery 

A sales model is defined by: 
  ‣ How demand is created 
  ‣ How price is constructed 
  ‣ How orders are placed 
  ‣ How revenue is recognized  Not by org charts. 

Examples of sales models you identify in Discovery 

During Discovery, you explicitly surface questions like: 

Who sells and how 


  ‣ Direct sales 
  ‣ Inside sales 
  ‣ Partner / reseller 
  ‣ Self-service / digital 

How pricing is constructed 


  ‣ List → discount 
  ‣ Contracted pricing 
  ‣ Usage-based pricing 
  ‣ Tiered / volume pricing 
  ‣ Bundle-based pricing 

How customers buy 


  ‣ One-time purchase 
  ‣ Subscription 
  ‣ Usage / consumption 
  ‣ Hybrid (subscription + usage) 
  ‣ Amendment-heavy vs straight-through 

How revenue is recognized 


  ‣ Point-in-time 
  ‣ Over-time 
  ‣ Milestone-based 
  ‣ Variable consideration  (This is critical in RLM.) 

How fulfillment happens 


  ‣ Immediate digital delivery 
  ‣ Physical shipment 
  ‣ Project-based delivery 
  ‣ Ongoing service 

Discovery deliverable (important) 

Discovery produces a Sales Model Inventory, not solutions.  Example output:  “The platform must support: 
  ‣ Direct enterprise subscription sales with amendments 
  ‣ Partner-led resale with net pricing 
  ‣ Usage-based add-ons billed monthly 
  ‣ One-time services recognized on milestones”  No config decisions yet. 

Why this must happen in Discovery (home analogy) 

This is equivalent to: 
  ‣ Deciding whether the land will support:  A single-family home  A duplex  A mixed-use building  You are not drawing the house yet —  you are deciding what kinds of buildings must legally stand on this land. 

What happens if you push this to Design (bad outcome) 

If sales models are discovered late: 
  ‣ CPQ redesign 
  ‣ Billing rework 
  ‣ Revenue recognition corrections 
  ‣ Contract remediation  This is the #1 cause of RLM program overruns.

Vision statement typically set before an it implementation of erp or RLM begins. Who does this 

In a correctly run ERP or Revenue Lifecycle (RLM) program, the Vision is not set by IT — and not by a single person either.  It is owned by the business, with IT / Architecture acting as translators and guardians.  Here’s the clean, enterprise-accurate answer. 

Short, precise answer 

The Vision is set by senior business leadership (ELT/SLT), often initiated by Finance or Revenue leadership, and formalized with Enterprise Architecture and IT.  IT implements.  Architecture translates.  Business owns intent. 

Who actually contributes (reality, not theory) 

Executive Sponsor (required) 

Usually one of: 
  ‣ CFO (very common for ERP / RLM) 
  ‣ CRO / Chief Revenue Officer 
  ‣ COO  They: 
  ‣ Own why the program exists 
  ‣ Define success criteria 
  ‣ Provide authority  Without this role, vision is weak or tactical. 

Business Leadership (co-authors) 

Functional heads from: 
  ‣ Sales 
  ‣ Finance 
  ‣ Legal / Compliance 
  ‣ Operations  They: 
  ‣ State business pain and goals 
  ‣ Set guardrails (compliance, risk) 
  ‣ Agree on what “good” looks like  This is where commercial intent comes from. 

Enterprise Architecture / Lead Architect (critical) 

Often invisible but essential.  They: 
  ‣ Keep vision at the right abstraction 
  ‣ Prevent solution leakage 
  ‣ Ensure vision is implementable 
  ‣ Translate business language → system-neutral intent  A good architect will say:  “That’s design, not vision.”

  ‣ CIO / IT Director 
  ‣ Platform owners  They: 
  ‣ Provide feasibility input

Good consultants:

  ‣ Insert their product as vision

I understand what you are saying, my question was whether companies work with different kind of consulting to arrive at this vision 

Yes — many companies do work with consultants to arrive at the Vision, but how they do it (and who) matters a lot.  Here’s the accurate, non-idealized reality. 

Short answer (clear and honest) 

Yes. Companies often engage strategy, transformation, or architecture consultants to facilitate and shape the Vision — but the Vision must still be owned and ratified by business leadership.  Consultants help articulate.  They should not own it. 

The types of consulting involved (this is the key) 

Strategy consulting (why-change vision) 

Examples: 
  ‣ McKinsey, BCG, Bain 
  ‣ Tier-2 strategy boutiques  They help answer: 
  ‣ Why now? 
  ‣ What business outcomes matter? 
  ‣ How revenue model or operating model should evolve?  This is business vision, not IT vision.  Used when: 
  ‣ Large transformation 
  ‣ Market or revenue model shift 
  ‣ M&A-driven complexity 

Business / Transformation consulting (operating model vision) 

Examples: 
  ‣ Accenture, Deloitte, PwC, EY 
  ‣ Transformation boutiques  They help: 
  ‣ Translate strategy into operating intent 
  ‣ Align Sales, Finance, Ops 
  ‣ Define guardrails  This is where Revenue Lifecycle Vision often emerges. 

Enterprise Architecture consulting (structure & abstraction) 

Examples: 
  ‣ EA boutiques 
  ‣ Senior independent architects 
  ‣ Internal EA teams with advisors  They: 
  ‣ Keep vision system-neutral 
  ‣ Prevent tool bias 
  ‣ Ensure future scalability  This role is often underappreciated but crucial. 

Vendor / platform consulting (should not define vision) 

Examples: 
  ‣ Salesforce SIs 
  ‣ ERP vendors  They: 
  ‣ Provide feasibility input 
  ‣ Share best practices  But when vendors define vision: 
  ‣ The vision becomes the product 
  ‣ Scope is distorted 

How this usually plays out in real companies 

Common (good) pattern  Strategy / transformation consultant frames why  Business leaders align on outcomes  EA helps articulate vision cleanly  Vendors are brought in after vision  Very common (problematic) pattern  Vendor brought in early  Vision becomes “implement X cloud”  Discovery is rushed  Rework later 

Why companies still use consultants (even strong ones) 

Because: 
  ‣ Internal leaders lack time 
  ‣ Neutral facilitation is needed 
  ‣ Cross-functional alignment is hard 
  ‣ Someone has to ask uncomfortable questions  Consultants provide structure and air cover.
